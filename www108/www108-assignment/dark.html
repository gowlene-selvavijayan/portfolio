Dark Mode vs. Dark Patterns in UX Design

Dark Mode

I'm a dark mode fanatic. From reducing eye strain to saving battery life with less energy consumption devices with OLED screens to minimizing visual noise and creating a more focused, aesthetic feel, the rising popularity of dark mode over the past decade is a no-brainer.

What I Learned

When exploring articles on dark mode in UX design, I learnt a lot about its accessibility concerns, benefits and best practices, including a few things I had not thought about.

Here are some of the other takeaways that stuck with me:

Benefits

  • Creating a Visual Hierarchy

    The background colour and surface colours of a dark theme can help guide users through a clean, cohesive visual hierarchy when planned and executed with intention. Paying attention to various layers of an interface, its foreground and background and creating elevation for a seamless user experience is, in my opinion, key to mastering dark mode design.

  • Branding Dark Mode

    Instead of grounding dark themes on a black or grey background, thinking about how to leverage use a darker shade of another colour to put out a unique brand identity or aesthetic can make dark mode design leave a lasting impression.

Best Practices

  • Adjusting Images

    Saturated images can cause uncomfortable contrasts against the dim lighting of dark themes. Desaturating images or decreasing the brightness and contrast of images, can help create a smoother, more comfortable user experience and reduce eye strain.

  • Background and Foreground Contrast

    Avoid using pure black for background or pure white for foreground elements. The high contrast can create a jarring visual experience for users, cause eye strain and impede readability. Many of the articles I came across recommended the use of softer shades of black or dark grey for backgrounds and dimmer whites or light greys for text to create a more balanced contrast while keeping accessibility in mind. They also pointed out the importance of paying attention to font weights as there is but a fine line between being too bold or too light and affecting readability in dark mode.

How I Applied It

Although I did not add a switch for users to toggle between light mode and dark mode in my design for this assignment, after doing research, I'm of the opinion that it is best to include a toggle switch AND use prefers-color-scheme media queries to set defaults based on the user's device settings. This way the user gets more control over their experience and can toggle between the two options with ease, while also having the design respond to their default preferences.

In my dark mode design, I paid close attention to uncomfortable contrasts. I used the CSS filter() function to decrease brightness, contrast and saturation across images. I made sure to not use pure white or black as much as possible, especially not in prominent elements, and worked to create a balanced, comfortable visual experience for the user.

I also wanted to create a unique aesthetic feel that reflected the colour palette of the TV Series I was exploring. So I decided to root my dark theme in a dark indigo background instead of a dark grey. As per some of the recommendations I came across when researching, all other background and foreground elements were typically shades of each other to create cohesiveness. I also used purple accents to tie it all together.

Dark Patterns

Dark patterns, or deceptive patterns, refers to design that exploits the way users intuitively use and interact with websites and apps to trick, manipulate or heavily influence users to make certain choices or perform actions they otherwise would not. From making users feel ashamed or confused, dark patterns in design persuade and push users to act in ways that go against their intentions or best interests. Widely used from e-commerce retailers and travel services to social media companies, numerous websites and apps use dark patterns to coerce or deceive users into giving away their data, money, or time, usually to benefit the company employing the pattern.

Users that feel deceived, manipulated, or misled often feel regretful, foolish, frustrated or dissatisfied. This can even lead to a complete lack of trust in the people, business or organization behind the design.

Some examples of dark patterns include sneaking in hidden costs or extra items at checkout, forced continuity by automatically charging after a free trial for a subscription service ends, using design elements to confuse or distract the user from achieving what they set out to do, or shaming and making users feel bad about saying no to sharing their data or subscribing to emails.

Confirm-Shaming

This dark pattern rooted in making users feel guilty or foolish has become a popular marketing tactic in recent years. Its intention is to get the user to opt into doing something like sharing their email and signing up for a newsletter, or prevent them from opting out of something (like unsubscribing from emails) by shaming the user from making the alternative choice.
